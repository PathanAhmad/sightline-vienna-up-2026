# Sightline — 3-minute pitch speech

For: Vienna UP 2026 final pitch · Sunday · Challenge 2.

**The rule of this speech:** the slide is for them. *You* are for them
too — but you say what their eyes can't see. The slide carries the
headline; your voice carries the meaning, the stakes, the story.

The slide-1 quote is the **only** thing you'll say that's also on a
slide — doubling is deliberate, so the line lands twice. Every other
slide: you gesture at it, then talk about something that isn't on it.

**Arc:**
1. **HOOK** — the quote (≈ 25 s)
2. **PIPELINE** — six stages, two surprises, eight checks (≈ 32 s)
3. → **LIVE DEMO** on the dashboard (≈ 85 s)
4. **NUMBERS + DIAL + BUSINESS** — two tiers, accuracy, alternative cost (≈ 38 s)
5. **CLOSE** — echo of the hook (≈ 12 s)

**Budget:** 3 minutes hard cap. Pauses are part of the budget. Use them.
**Beat 4 of the demo is no longer optional** — it's the only place the
audience sees the cost/quality dial on a real surface, and Slide 3
references it. If you must cut, trim 5 s from Beat 1 and 3 s from the
hook instead.

---

## Before you walk on

Three breaths. Phone on silent. Laptop on the demo URL already loaded —
don't trust the wifi to load it cold. The first line of slide 1 in your
head once. You already know the rest.

---

## SLIDE 1 — the hook  (≈ 25 seconds)

*[Walk to centre. Slow. Don't start until the room is still. Look up.
One beat. Then deliver the quote — quietly, the way you'd say something
that matters to a friend.]*

> **"The trench gets filled in.**
> **The questions never do."**

*[Two-beat pause. Let it land. Don't rush.]*

A year from now, an excavator hits a buried cable. Two years from now,
an auditor asks who signed off. Five years from now, a regulator wants
the record.

Every one of those questions ends up in the same place — a folder full
of photos that nobody has time to read.

*[Half-beat.]*

We're Sightline. We read it.

*[Click to slide 2.]*

---

## SLIDE 2 — the pipeline  (≈ 35 seconds)

*[The slide shows six stages: INGEST → DEDUP → READ → SCORE → MAP →
REPORT. **Don't read the labels off the screen.** Glance at the slide,
then look at the room and tell them the parts that surprised you.]*

Here's the pipeline. Six stages — two surprised us most.

*[Beat. Then start. Use a slower cadence for "First / Second" — these
are your two technical money-shots.]*

**First** — we **dedup before the AI sees anything**. Perceptual
hashing on a laptop. Free. We caught about six hundred duplicates
*before* spending a cent on the model. Engineering choice — not luck.

*[Beat.]*

**Second** — there is **no GPS in these photos**. WhatsApp strips it on
upload. Zero out of two hundred photos we sampled. So we read the
location from text *printed on the photo itself* — four number formats,
three languages, sometimes Cyrillic and Latin in the same line. One
vision call handles all of it.

*[Brief pause, then quick wrap-up — these don't need elaboration.
Name the checks one breath, don't slow down.]*

The rest of the line: eight checks on every photo — depth reference,
warning tape, sand bedding, sealed cable ends, side-view framing,
faces or plates for privacy, duplicate fingerprints, GPS-versus-
address sanity. Every photo pinned to its five-meter trench segment.
CSV and PDF out the back end.

Let me show you.

*[Alt-tab to the dashboard.]*

## DEMO — the dashboard  (≈ 90 seconds)

*Four beats. Talk while you click. Don't narrate mouse movements —
narrate **findings**.*

**Beat 1 — the map (15 s).**
> "Maria Rain — a Carinthian fiber-trench project. Every stretch of
> trench coloured. Green is documented. Red is where it isn't."

**Beat 2 — click a red segment (25 s).**
> "This one — no depth reference, no warning tape, and one of the only
> photos has a worker's face in frame. Privacy-flagged — can't ship.
> Two photos across thirty meters of trench. A reviewer goes here
> first."

**Beat 3 — the duplicate trail (25 s) — your headline moment.**
> "And this. Same photo, three different jobs. Caught automatically.
> The original, the copies, and which contractor put it where."
*[Pause. Let the room react. Don't talk over the reaction.]*

**Beat 4 — upload view, MANDATORY (15 s) — the dial proof.**
> "And the operator side — drop a fresh photo, eight checks in six
> seconds. *[Point at the Sonnet/Haiku toggle.]* And there's the dial
> — fast tier or careful tier, operator picks. Catches the gap while
> the trench is still open."

**Closing the demo (5 s).**
> "Whole project — two numbers on the next slide. Pick yours."

*[Alt-tab back to the deck. You'll land on slide 3 — Numbers.]*

---

## SLIDE 3 — numbers + the dial  (≈ 28 seconds)

*[The slide shows the dataset size, the two-tier cost/time, and the
accuracy callout. Don't read what's on the slide — let it work as a
visual anchor while you talk around it.]*

Thirty-nine hundred photos.

*[Beat. Half-second. Then — slower cadence, this is the dial moment.]*

Twelve dollars and ninety minutes on the fast tier.
Forty dollars and three hours on the careful tier.

*[Beat.]*

Same pipeline. The operator picks.

*[Full beat. Let the dial land before the credibility line.]*

And we measured the difference. Two hundred and fourteen photos,
hand-labeled, scored blind. On the safety-critical shots — the ones
with a depth reference — the careful tier agrees with the human label
ninety-six percent of the time. The fast tier, eighty-seven. Across
the harder class we land in the low seventies overall. We know where
each tier is strong, and where it isn't.

*[Half-beat. Shift tone — quieter, factual. This is the buyer's
question and they want to hear it answered without a sales voice.]*

The manual alternative is three to five engineer-days per section.
We replace weeks of senior review time with the price of a lunch.
Sold as a service, or licensed into an operator's stack — same math
either way.

*[Click to slide 4.]*

---

## SLIDE 4 — close  (≈ 15 seconds)

*[Step back from the laptop. Look at the room, not the screen. Slow
down — this is the line they remember.]*

Once the trench is filled, the photos are all you have.

*[Beat. Then warmer, action-oriented:]*

If you've got thirty seconds after this — come click anything in the
dashboard. The only thing we haven't tested yet is what **you** can
break.

*[Small nod. Stop talking. Don't say "thank you." Don't fade out.
Just end.]*

## Stage notes — read these once before you go on

1. **The slide-1 quote is the only thing you'll say that's on a slide.**
   Everywhere else, the slide handles the structure; you handle the
   meaning. If you find yourself reading the screen, stop and look at
   the room.
2. **Slide 2 is your tech-credibility moment.** 30 % of the score is
   "tech & AI." Use the **First / Second** structure — pause clearly
   between them. Each one is a small "huh" moment for the audience.
3. **Pauses are not empty space.** They are how the room knows you
   meant something. Don't fill them.
4. **The duplicate-detection moment in the demo is your headline.**
   That visual is what the judges will remember. Pause for one full
   beat after "the original, the copies, and which contractor put it
   where" — let them process before you move on.
5. **On slide 3, the dial is the structural beat — not the dollar
   amount.** Say the dataset size, then the two tiers in matched
   cadence: "*Twelve dollars and ninety minutes on the fast tier.
   Forty dollars and three hours on the careful tier.*" The contrast
   is what they remember. Then the "operator picks" line lands the
   product point.
6. **The per-photo cost is measured. The project total is arithmetic.**
   The PDF in `data/model_comparison.pdf` is your receipt — 214 hand-
   labeled photos, two models, same prompt, same 8-worker pool. If a
   judge asks how we know the project numbers: "*Per-photo cost and
   wall-time are benchmarked. Project total is per-photo times photo
   count.*" Don't say "projected" — say "benchmarked." Confident, with
   the PDF as the backstop if they want to see it.
7. **The accuracy beat is your honesty moment — and it's now two
   tiers, not one.** "Ninety-six percent careful tier, eighty-seven
   percent fast tier — both on the safety-critical depth-evidence
   shots. Low seventies across the harder class." Naming both tiers
   AND the weak spot is what makes this beat work. Don't drop any of
   the three.
8. **The business-model beat doesn't pitch a price.** It pitches
   the *alternative's* cost (3-5 engineer-days per section) and
   leaves the math implicit. "*Sold as a service, or licensed into
   an operator's stack*" covers both delivery modes — standalone
   product or absorbed into a buyer's tooling — without committing
   to either. If a judge presses on price after the pitch, the honest
   answer is "depends on scale and delivery mode; the cloud cost is
   in the PDF." Don't quote a markup. Anchor on the manual baseline
   and let them multiply.
8. **No apologies. No "we tried." No qualifiers** *outside the accuracy
   beat*. You shipped a working system in 48 hours. Stand on it. The
   one place you *do* qualify is the accuracy moment — and that's
   deliberate honesty, not a flinch.
9. **If the wifi dies mid-demo:** switch to the backup video without
   breaking stride. Say "and here's the same flow on a second
   dataset" — keep going.
10. **Don't say "thank you" at the end.** Closing with "thank you"
    tells the room the pitch is over and lowers your status. End on
    "what you can break." Nod. Walk off. The nod does the thank-you's
    job better.
